# One-on-One Meetings

## Overall Summary

This lesson covers the structure and purpose of effective one-on-one meetings between managers and direct reports. It distinguishes one-on-ones from status updates, presents a repeatable meeting framework, and addresses common pitfalls that make one-on-ones feel like a waste of time.

## Key Points

- One-on-ones belong to the direct report, not the manager — let them set the agenda
- The ideal cadence is weekly for 30 minutes, biweekly at minimum
- Three pillars: relationship building, obstacle removal, and growth conversations
- Active listening means reflecting back, not problem-solving immediately
- Document action items and follow up — nothing kills trust faster than forgotten commitments

## Detailed Notes

### What One-on-Ones Are NOT

- Status updates (use standups or async tools for that)
- Performance reviews (those happen quarterly/annually)
- Manager's chance to assign more work
- Optional meetings that get cancelled when things are busy

### The 30-Minute Framework

1. **Check-in (5 min)**: How are you doing? Anything on your mind?
2. **Their agenda (15 min)**: Topics the direct report wants to discuss
3. **Your topics (5 min)**: Things the manager needs to bring up
4. **Action items (5 min)**: What are we each committing to?

### Common Pitfalls

- **Cancelling regularly**: Signals that the relationship isn't a priority
- **Doing all the talking**: Aim for 70/30 — direct report talks 70% of the time
- **Only discussing tasks**: Missing the relationship and growth dimensions
- **Not following up**: Action items from last meeting should be the first checkpoint
